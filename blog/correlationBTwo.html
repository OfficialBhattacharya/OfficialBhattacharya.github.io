4. What is the correlation coefficient for an equilateral triangle in the first quadrant in XY plane?

The Problem

Consider an equilateral triangle in the XY plane:

  • Three vertices serve as data points
  • The triangle can rotate around its centroid
  • We want to understand how the regression line changes with rotation

Initial Setup

For an equilateral triangle centered at the origin (0,0), the vertices can be represented as:

$$ (x_k, y_k) = \left(r\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{3} + \theta\right), r\sin\left(\frac{2\pi k}{3} + \theta\right)\right) $$

where:

  • r is the distance from the centroid to any vertex.
  • The rotation angle: $$\theta$$
  • k = 0,1,2 represents the three vertices.

Observations from Regression Line Properties

Constant Slope

  • The regression line is always parallel to the X-axis.
  • The slope is always zero.
  • $$\therefore y = \bar{y}$$

Centroid Property

  • The regression line always passes through the centroid.
  • Since the triangle is centered at the origin, the line is y = 0.

Proof

1. Mean Values

For any rotation angle $$\theta$$:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=0}^{2} r\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{3} + \theta\right) = 0 $$ $$ \bar{y} = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=0}^{2} r\sin\left(\frac{2\pi k}{3} + \theta\right) = 0 $$

2. Correlation Coefficient

The correlation between x and y coordinates is:

$$ \rho_{x,y} = \frac{\sum_{k=0}^{2} (x_k - \bar{x})(y_k - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{k=0}^{2} (x_k - \bar{x})^2 \sum_{k=0}^{2} (y_k - \bar{y})^2}} = 0 $$

3. Regression Line

$$\because \rho_{x,y} = 0$$ The regression line is:

$$ y = \bar{y} = 0 $$

Implications

Geometric Interpretation

1. Symmetry

  • The equilateral triangle's perfect symmetry ensures equal distribution of points.
  • Any rotation preserves this symmetry.
  • The centroid remains fixed at the origin.

2. Independence?

  • The x and y coordinates are uncorrelated.
  • There is no linear relationship between x and y.
  • This holds true for any rotation angle.

5. Can we extend this to regular polygons using complex roots of unity or otherwise?

Complex Roots of Unity

The n-th roots of unity are the complex numbers that satisfy the equation:

$$ z^n = 1 $$

These roots can be expressed in the form:

$$ z_k = e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{n}}, \quad k = 0,1,\ldots,n-1 $$

where:

  • i is the imaginary unit.
  • n is the number of roots.
  • k is the index of each root.

Geometric Interpretation

When plotted on the complex plane:

  • Each root lies on the unit circle.
  • The roots are evenly spaced, forming the vertices of a regular n-gon.
  • The first root (k=0) is always at (1,0).
  • The roots are distributed at angles of $$\frac{2\pi k}{n}$$ from the positive real axis.

Key Properties

The sum of all n-th roots of unity is zero:

$$ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{n}} = 0 $$

This implies that both the real and imaginary parts sum to zero:

$$ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = 0 $$ $$ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = 0 $$

Coordinate Representation

For a regular n-gon, the vertices can be represented as:

$$ (x_k, y_k) = \left(\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right), \sin\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right)\right) $$

Zero Correlation

Mean Values

From the properties of roots of unity:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = 0 $$ $$ \bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \sin\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = 0 $$

Simplified Correlation Formula

$$\because \bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0,$$ the correlation formula simplifies to:

$$\therefore \rho_{x,y} = \frac{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} x_k y_k}{\sqrt{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} x_k^2 \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} y_k^2}} $$

Trigonometric Identities

The numerator becomes:

$$ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) \sin\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = 0 $$

The denominator terms are equal:

$$ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \cos^2\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \sin^2\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) = \frac{n}{2} $$

Final Result

$$ \therefore \rho_{x,y} = \frac{0}{\sqrt{\frac{n}{2} \cdot \frac{n}{2}}} = 0 $$

Relatively New Measures of Independence/Correlation

While Pearson's correlation has been the gold standard for decades, recent developments have introduced more robust and comprehensive measures of dependence. Here are some cutting-edge approaches:

1. Chatterjee's Correlation Coefficient

A new coefficient of correlation by Sourav Chatterjee represents a significant breakthrough in correlation analysis:

  • Chatterjee's correlation is distribution-free, asymptotically normal, and consistent against all alternatives.
  • It is designed to be more robust to outliers and non-linear relationships compared to Pearson's and Spearman's.
  • This measure is particularly useful when the underlying distribution is unknown or when dealing with complex relationships.
  • Unlike Pearson's correlation, it can detect any type of functional relationship, not just linear ones.

2. Nonlinear Correlation Coefficient (NCC)

The NCC provides a measure that captures nonlinear dependencies more effectively:

  • Specifically designed to detect nonlinear relationships that traditional correlation measures miss.
  • Provides a more comprehensive view of variable relationships.
  • Particularly useful in machine learning and data mining applications.

3. Fechner Correlation Coefficient

A robust alternative that:

  • Is less sensitive to outliers than Pearson's correlation.
  • Provides insights into monotonic relationships.
  • Offers computational advantages in certain scenarios.

4. g-Correlation Coefficient

A generalized approach that:

  • Extends traditional correlation concepts to more complex relationships.
  • Provides flexibility in capturing various types of dependence structures.
  • Offers theoretical foundations for understanding complex data relationships.

Conclusion

In this two-part series, we've explored some fascinating aspects of correlation and statistical dependence. From the fundamental properties of Pearson's correlation to the geometric elegance of regular polygons, and finally to the cutting-edge developments in correlation measures, we've seen how mathematics continues to evolve to meet the challenges of modern data analysis.

The key takeaways from our exploration:

  • Zero correlation does not imply independence in general
  • The sum of marginally normal variables is not always normal
  • Regular polygons exhibit beautiful symmetry properties in their correlation structure
  • New correlation measures are expanding our toolkit for understanding complex relationships

These insights remind us that in statistics, as in many fields, the most interesting discoveries often lie in the exceptions to the rules and the exploration of edge cases that challenge our assumptions.
