Asset-Liability Management (ALM): Example of Gap Analysis

June 1, 2025 By Diganta Bhattacharya

Gap Analysis and simulation modeling are critical tools in Asset-Liability Management (ALM) for assessing interest rate risk and liquidity risk. This comprehensive framework enables financial institutions to manage their balance sheet exposures and optimize their risk-return profile.

Step 1: Perform Gap Analysis

  1. Define Time Buckets: Divide the time horizon into buckets (e.g., 0–1 month, 1–3 months, 3–6 months, 6–12 months, 1–5 years, >5 years).
  2. Classify Assets and Liabilities: Categorize all assets and liabilities into the time buckets based on their repricing or maturity dates.
  3. Calculate Gaps: For each time bucket, compute the repricing gap:
    \[ \text{Gap}_i = \text{Assets}_i - \text{Liabilities}_i \]
    Where:
    • \(\text{Assets}_i\): Assets repricing or maturing in bucket \(i\)
    • \(\text{Liabilities}_i\): Liabilities repricing or maturing in bucket \(i\)
  4. Cumulative Gap: Compute the cumulative gap up to each bucket:
    \[ \text{Cumulative Gap}_i = \sum_{j=1}^i \text{Gap}_j \]
  5. Interpret Gaps:
    • Positive gap: More assets than liabilities are repricing (benefits from rising rates)
    • Negative gap: More liabilities than assets are repricing (benefits from falling rates)

Step 2: Simulate Interest Rate Scenarios

  1. Define Scenarios: Specify interest rate scenarios (e.g., parallel shifts, steepening, flattening, or custom yield curve changes).
  2. Simulate Rate Changes: For each scenario, adjust the yield curve by the specified shifts.
  3. Revalue Assets and Liabilities: Revalue all assets and liabilities under the new yield curve using discounted cash flow (DCF) methods:
    \[ PV = \sum_{t=1}^T \frac{CF_t}{(1 + r_t)^t} \]
    Where:
    • \(CF_t\): Cash flow at time \(t\)
    • \(r_t\): Discount rate at time \(t\)

Step 3: Assess Impact on Net Interest Income (NII)

  1. Project Cash Flows: For each scenario, project cash flows from assets and liabilities based on their repricing schedules.
  2. Calculate NII: Compute the net interest income for each scenario:
    \[ \text{NII} = \sum_{i=1}^n (\text{Interest Income}_i - \text{Interest Expense}_i) \]
    Where:
    • \(\text{Interest Income}_i\): Interest income from assets in bucket \(i\)
    • \(\text{Interest Expense}_i\): Interest expense from liabilities in bucket \(i\)
  3. Compare Scenarios: Compare NII across scenarios to assess the impact of interest rate changes.

Step 4: Assess Impact on Economic Value of Equity (EVE)

  1. Revalue Equity: Compute the economic value of equity (EVE) under each scenario:
    \[ \text{EVE} = \text{PV of Assets} - \text{PV of Liabilities} \]
  2. Sensitivity Analysis: Analyze the change in EVE across scenarios to assess interest rate risk.

Step 5: Simulate Liquidity Scenarios

  1. Define Liquidity Scenarios: Specify scenarios for changes in funding availability or deposit withdrawals.
  2. Project Cash Flows: For each scenario, project cash inflows and outflows based on the liquidity profile of assets and liabilities.
  3. Calculate Liquidity Gaps: Compute the liquidity gap for each time bucket:
    \[ \text{Liquidity Gap}_i = \text{Cash Inflows}_i - \text{Cash Outflows}_i \]
  4. Cumulative Liquidity Gap: Compute the cumulative liquidity gap up to each bucket:
    \[ \text{Cumulative Liquidity Gap}_i = \sum_{j=1}^i \text{Liquidity Gap}_j \]
  5. Assess Liquidity Risk: Identify periods of liquidity shortfalls and plan contingency funding.

Step 6: Validate Results

  1. Consistency Check: Ensure the gap analysis and simulation results are consistent with historical data and market conditions.
  2. Stress Testing: Perform stress tests for extreme scenarios (e.g., large rate shocks, severe liquidity crises).

Mathematical Summary

  1. Repricing gap:
    \[ \text{Gap}_i = \text{Assets}_i - \text{Liabilities}_i \]
  2. Cumulative gap:
    \[ \text{Cumulative Gap}_i = \sum_{j=1}^i \text{Gap}_j \]
  3. Net interest income:
    \[ \text{NII} = \sum_{i=1}^n (\text{Interest Income}_i - \text{Interest Expense}_i) \]
  4. Economic value of equity:
    \[ \text{EVE} = \text{PV of Assets} - \text{PV of Liabilities} \]
  5. Liquidity gap:
    \[ \text{Liquidity Gap}_i = \text{Cash Inflows}_i - \text{Cash Outflows}_i \]

Step 7: Interpretation

  1. Gap Analysis:
    • Identify mismatches in repricing and maturity profiles
    • Plan hedging strategies to mitigate interest rate risk
    • Assess the balance sheet's sensitivity to rate changes
  2. Simulation Results:
    • Assess the impact of interest rate and liquidity scenarios on NII and EVE
    • Develop contingency plans for adverse scenarios
    • Optimize asset-liability mix for risk-adjusted returns

Regulatory Framework and Best Practices

  1. Regulatory Requirements:
    • Comply with Basel III requirements for interest rate risk in the banking book (IRRBB)
    • Meet local regulatory standards for ALM and stress testing
    • Maintain adequate capital buffers based on ALM analysis
  2. Governance Framework:
    • Establish clear roles and responsibilities for ALM
    • Implement regular reporting to senior management and board
    • Maintain independent validation of ALM models and assumptions
  3. Model Risk Management:
    • Validate behavioral assumptions for non-maturity deposits
    • Back-test model predictions against actual outcomes
    • Document model limitations and uncertainty ranges

Advanced ALM Techniques

  1. Dynamic Hedging:
    • Implement dynamic hedging strategies based on gap analysis
    • Use derivatives to manage interest rate and liquidity risk
    • Monitor hedge effectiveness and adjust positions regularly
  2. Behavioral Modeling:
    • Model customer behavior for deposits and loan prepayments
    • Incorporate option values in behavioral models
    • Account for competitive dynamics in pricing models
  3. Multi-Currency ALM:
    • Extend gap analysis to multiple currencies
    • Manage foreign exchange risk alongside interest rate risk
    • Consider currency basis risk in funding strategies

Practical Implementation

A typical ALM framework implementation involves:

  • Data Infrastructure: Robust data collection and validation processes
  • Technology Platform: Integrated ALM systems with scenario modeling capabilities
  • Risk Limits: Clear limits on gap exposures and risk metrics
  • Reporting Framework: Regular management reports with key risk indicators
  • Strategic Planning: Integration with business planning and capital allocation
