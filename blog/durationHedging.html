Hedging MBS using Duration: Example of Hedge Ratio Calculation

June 22, 2025 By Diganta Bhattacharya

To calculate the duration of an MBS portfolio and a hedging instrument (e.g., Treasury bonds), and determine the hedge ratio, we need to understand the interest rate sensitivity of both the portfolio and the hedging instruments. This comprehensive approach enables effective risk management of mortgage-backed securities portfolios.

Step 1: Calculate the Duration of the MBS Portfolio

  1. Cash Flow Projection: Project the cash flows of the MBS portfolio, incorporating prepayment assumptions (e.g., using CPR or a custom prepayment model).
  2. Discount Cash Flows: Discount the cash flows using the appropriate yield curve:
    \[ PV_t = \frac{CF_t}{(1 + r_t)^t} \]
    Where:
    • \(CF_t\): Cash flow at time \(t\)
    • \(r_t\): Discount rate at time \(t\)
  3. Calculate Macaulay Duration: Compute the weighted average time to receive cash flows:
    \[ D_{\text{MBS}} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T t \cdot PV_t}{\sum_{t=1}^T PV_t} \]
  4. Calculate Modified Duration: Adjust for changes in yield:
    \[ \text{Modified Duration}_{\text{MBS}} = \frac{D_{\text{MBS}}}{1 + \frac{y}{m}} \]
    Where:
    • \(y\): Yield to maturity
    • \(m\): Number of compounding periods per year

Step 2: Calculate the Duration of the Hedging Instrument

  1. Cash Flow Projection: Project the cash flows of the hedging instrument (e.g., Treasury bonds).
  2. Discount Cash Flows: Discount the cash flows using the appropriate yield curve:
    \[ PV_t = \frac{CF_t}{(1 + r_t)^t} \]
  3. Calculate Macaulay Duration: Compute the weighted average time to receive cash flows:
    \[ D_{\text{Hedge}} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T t \cdot PV_t}{\sum_{t=1}^T PV_t} \]
  4. Calculate Modified Duration: Adjust for changes in yield:
    \[ \text{Modified Duration}_{\text{Hedge}} = \frac{D_{\text{Hedge}}}{1 + \frac{y}{m}} \]

Step 3: Determine the Hedge Ratio

  1. Market Values: Compute the market value of the MBS portfolio (\(V_{\text{MBS}}\)) and the hedging instrument (\(V_{\text{Hedge}}\)).
  2. Hedge Ratio: Calculate the hedge ratio (\(HR\)) as:
    \[ HR = \frac{\text{Modified Duration}_{\text{MBS}} \times V_{\text{MBS}}}{\text{Modified Duration}_{\text{Hedge}} \times V_{\text{Hedge}}} \]

Step 4: Validate the Hedge

  1. Sensitivity Analysis: Test the hedge ratio under different yield curve scenarios (e.g., parallel shifts, steepening, flattening).
  2. Rebalancing: Monitor the hedge ratio over time and rebalance as needed to maintain effective hedging.

Mathematical Summary

  1. Macaulay duration:
    \[ D = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T t \cdot PV_t}{\sum_{t=1}^T PV_t} \]
  2. Modified duration:
    \[ \text{Modified Duration} = \frac{D}{1 + \frac{y}{m}} \]
  3. Hedge ratio:
    \[ HR = \frac{\text{Modified Duration}_{\text{MBS}} \times V_{\text{MBS}}}{\text{Modified Duration}_{\text{Hedge}} \times V_{\text{Hedge}}} \]

Step 5: Interpretation

  1. Hedge Ratio:
    • The hedge ratio indicates the number of units of the hedging instrument required to offset the interest rate risk of the MBS portfolio
    • A hedge ratio of 1 implies a perfect hedge under small yield changes
    • Hedge ratios greater than 1 indicate that more hedging instruments are needed than the portfolio value
  2. Effectiveness:
    • The hedge is effective if the portfolio's value remains stable under yield curve changes
    • Perfect hedging assumes parallel yield curve shifts and linear price-yield relationships
    • Real-world effectiveness may vary due to basis risk and convexity differences

Advanced Hedging Considerations

  1. Convexity Adjustment: For large yield changes, incorporate convexity differences:
    \[ \Delta P = -\text{Duration} \times \Delta y \times P + \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Convexity} \times (\Delta y)^2 \times P \]
    Where:
    • \(\Delta P\): Change in price
    • \(\Delta y\): Change in yield
    • \(P\): Initial price
  2. Key Rate Duration Hedging:
    • Use multiple hedging instruments to match key rate durations
    • Hedge specific yield curve segments (e.g., 2-year, 5-year, 10-year)
    • Minimize exposure to non-parallel yield curve shifts
  3. Option-Adjusted Duration:
    • Account for embedded options in MBS (prepayment options)
    • Use effective duration that incorporates option-adjusted spreads
    • Consider negative convexity effects at low interest rates

Hedging Instruments and Strategies

  1. Treasury Securities:
    • Treasury bonds and notes for long-term duration matching
    • Treasury futures for cost-effective hedging
    • STRIPS for zero-coupon duration matching
  2. Interest Rate Swaps:
    • Fixed-for-floating swaps to manage duration exposure
    • Asset swaps to hedge specific MBS tranches
    • Basis swaps to manage spread risk
  3. Options and Swaptions:
    • Interest rate caps and floors for asymmetric hedging
    • Swaptions to hedge prepayment option risk
    • Collar strategies for cost-effective hedging

Dynamic Hedging and Rebalancing

  1. Hedge Monitoring:
    • Track hedge effectiveness using P&L attribution
    • Monitor duration drift due to time decay and rate changes
    • Assess basis risk between MBS and hedging instruments

Risk Management Framework

  1. Risk Limits:
    • Set duration limits for the net portfolio exposure
    • Establish maximum hedge ratio deviations
    • Monitor value-at-risk (VaR) for the hedged portfolio
  2. Stress Testing:
    • Test hedge performance under extreme rate scenarios
    • Assess hedging effectiveness during market stress
    • Evaluate correlation breakdown risks
  3. Performance Attribution:
    • Decompose portfolio returns by duration risk and spread risk
    • Measure hedging effectiveness through tracking error
    • Analyze residual risks and unhedged exposures

Practical Implementation Example

Consider an MBS portfolio with the following characteristics:

  • Portfolio value: $100 million
  • Modified duration: 4.2 years
  • Hedging instrument: 10-year Treasury futures
  • Futures modified duration: 8.5 years
  • Futures contract value: $100,000

Calculate the hedge ratio:

\[ HR = \frac{4.2 \times 100,000,000}{8.5 \times 100,000} = \frac{420,000,000}{850,000} \approx 494 \text{ contracts} \]

This means approximately 494 Treasury futures contracts should be sold to hedge the interest rate risk of the $100 million MBS portfolio.
